The communication channel that enables coordination is also the channel through which regimes suppress it.
What if we could study this trap experimentally—running 45,000 decisions by AI agents who must coordinate under uncertainty, surveillance, and manipulation?
01 — The Game

A Country on the Edge

Imagine a country where citizens must independently decide: JOIN an uprising, or STAY home. The regime has some hidden strength θ. If enough people join—if the attack mass exceeds θ—the regime falls and joiners win. If not, the regime survives and joiners are punished.

Each citizen receives a noisy private signal about regime strength—delivered as a natural-language intelligence briefing. Not a number. Not a probability. Words.

8 evidence domains, 3 latent sliders, thousands of word-choice variations recover effective signal continuity from discrete text.

Global game theory (Morris & Shin 2003) predicts a unique threshold equilibrium: each citizen has a cutoff—join if your signal suggests the regime is weak enough, stay otherwise. The question: do LLMs find this equilibrium?

04 — Information Design

Redesigning the Signal

If LLMs play equilibrium, a social planner can exploit this. By redesigning the information structure—what agents see and how—the planner shifts outcomes without changing the underlying reality.

Baseline Signal

Standard information environment. Agents receive unbiased private signals about regime strength. The sigmoid follows the equilibrium prediction.

Stability Design

The planner dampens signals near the critical threshold θ*. Agents perceive the regime as stronger than it is. The sigmoid shifts right—fewer join.

The planner has multiple tools: censorship (suppress extreme signals), public signals (broadcast common information), and targeted instability (amplify anti-regime signals near θ*). Each reshapes the equilibrium differently—and LLM agents respond precisely as Bayesian persuasion theory predicts.

05 — Surveillance

The Chilling Effect

When agents are told they're being watched, something striking happens. Their beliefs barely change. But their actions change dramatically.

Citizens gather in the square. Some carry signs. Others speak openly about change. The crowd grows. Voices rise. Someone says the regime is weak. Another agrees. Then word spreads: the regime is listening.
< 1 pp shift
Beliefs
vs.
−13.5 pp shift
Actions

Second-order beliefs shift by less than 1 percentage point. Expressed joining drops by 13.5 pp. Agents self-censor while interpreting others' silence as genuine.

Each agent changes its own behavior—but believes
everyone else's silence is sincere.

This is the strategic update gap. The surveillance doesn't change what agents think. It changes what they do—and that silence propagates, because each agent reads others' compliance as genuine conviction. The regime doesn't need to change minds. It just needs to change the signal.

06 — Propaganda

Diminishing Returns of Lies

The regime can also plant propaganda agents in the communication round. But propaganda saturates: doubling planted agents from 5 to 10 adds exactly zero additional effect. And when combined with surveillance, the interaction is super-additive—the whole becomes worse than the sum of its parts.

07 — Conclusion
LLM agents don't just generate text that sounds strategic.
They play threshold strategies consistent with
Bayesian Nash Equilibrium—
and the information structures that theory says should
manipulate rational players work on them too.

The communication channel that enables coordination is also the channel through which regimes suppress it. This is not a metaphor. It is a measurable, manipulable equilibrium property.
