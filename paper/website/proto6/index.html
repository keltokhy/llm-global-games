A study of coordination, information design, and strategic self-censorship in LLM agents

Khaled Eltokhy · Department of Economics, The Graduate Center, CUNY

Abstract

The communication channel that enables coordination is also the channel through which regimes suppress it. I embed seven large language models in the Morris–Shin (2003) regime change global game, conveying private signals as natural-language intelligence briefings. Across 1,800 country-periods and 45,000 decisions, join rates track the theoretical attack mass with mean r = +0.80 (p < 0.001 for every model), collapse to r ≈ 0 under scrambled briefings, and invert to r = −0.72 under flipped signals.

The pattern reflects payoff structure, not sentiment: varying benefit/cost narratives while holding briefings fixed shifts cutoffs in lockstep with theory (r = +0.97)—a 50 percentage-point swing invisible to any text classifier. Surveillance poisons the communication channel: expressed joining falls by ~13.5 pp while second-order beliefs shift by less than 1 pp. Agents self-censor while interpreting others' silence as genuine: the regime prevents common knowledge of shared discontent from forming without changing the beliefs themselves.

Section I

The Information Channel Is a Trap

Imagine a country where citizens must independently decide whether to join an uprising or stay home. The regime has some hidden strength θ. If enough people join—if the attack mass exceeds θ—the regime falls and joiners win. If too few join, the regime survives and joiners are punished. Each citizen gets a noisy private signal about regime strength, delivered as a natural-language intelligence briefing.

The key insight from global game theory (Morris & Shin, 2003): under private information, there is a unique equilibrium in threshold strategies. Each citizen has a cutoff—join if your signal suggests the regime is weak enough, stay otherwise. The result is a sigmoid: the fraction of citizens who join traces an S-curve as a function of regime strength.

Coordination under uncertainty requires communication—but any channel that transmits willingness to act also transmits exploitable uncertainty about others' willingness to act.

This paper asks whether LLM agents implement these threshold strategies when signals arrive as text rather than numbers—and then exploits the regularity to study how an information designer can reshape coordination outcomes.

Section II

The Global Game of Regime Change

A continuum of citizens simultaneously choose whether to join an uprising or stay home. The regime falls if the mass of joiners exceeds the regime's strength θ. Payoffs are asymmetric: joining a successful revolution yields benefit B; joining a failed one costs C; staying home always pays zero.

Core Equations
Regime falls ⇔ A > θ   (attack mass exceeds strength)
Signal: xi = θ + εi,   ε ~ N(0, σ²)
Threshold: Join if xi < x*  where  x* = θ* + σΦ−1(θ*)
Attack mass: A(θ) = Φ((x* − θ) / σ)
Cutoff: θ* = B/(B+C) = 0.50  when B = C = 1

The attack mass A(θ) is the theoretical prediction: a sigmoid that decreases in regime strength. Weaker regimes face larger uprisings. The empirical question is whether LLM agents, reading natural-language briefings rather than numeric signals, produce join rates that track this theoretical curve.

Section III

They Do

Result 1 — Threshold-Policy Alignment

Across seven models and 1,800 country-periods, the Pearson correlation between the empirical join fraction and the theoretical attack mass averages r = +0.80 (p < 0.001 for every model).

The empirical sigmoid closely tracks the theoretical prediction. Correlations range from r = +0.75 (Mistral) to r = +0.84 (Trinity), with a pooled OLS R² = 0.58. Alignment is stable across architectures spanning 30B to 235B parameters: what varies across models is the mean join rate (an intercept shift reflecting model-specific action biases), not the correlation with theory.

Section IV

How Do We Know It's Real?

Five identification tests establish that the behavioral regularity reflects more than text classification. The most striking are the falsification treatments: scramble and flip.

The scramble preserves the marginal distribution of briefing content but breaks the mapping from each period's θ to agent signals. The flip preserves the mapping but reverses direction. Together, they establish that LLMs are reading signal content, not merely responding to surface sentiment.

The 50-percentage-point cost/benefit swing is invisible to any classifier conditioned on briefing text—the same words, different stakes, radically different behavior.

The cost/benefit sweep provides the sharpest test. Holding briefing text identical, varying only the narrative stakes (high cost vs. low cost) shifts mean joining by 50 percentage points. The fitted cutoff tracks the theoretical prediction with r = +0.97. A structural comparison against cognitive hierarchy benchmarks selects BNE (RMSE = 0.199) over Level-1 (RMSE = 0.307) and Level-2 (RMSE = 0.348). LLM agents approximate full Bayesian Nash Equilibrium reasoning—a striking departure from human laboratory subjects, who consistently exhibit level-k behavior.

Section V

Communication Opens a Channel

Before testing authoritarian instruments, I open a pre-play communication channel on a Watts–Strogatz small-world network. Citizens exchange messages before making their join/stay decisions. The result: communication preserves the signal structure (r = +0.78 vs. +0.80 under pure) while producing heterogeneous and near-zero average effects on join rates.

How informative are the messages? Regressing true θ on message features yields R² = 0.12—messages carry real information, noisily. This informativeness is precisely what makes the channel exploitable. Communication creates the information topology that authoritarian instruments attack.

Section VI

Reshaping the Information Environment

Result — Information Design Shifts Coordination

All information design families—stability, instability, public signal, and censorship—produce measurable shifts in coordination relative to baseline, consistent with Bayesian persuasion theory.

Taking the behavioral foundation as given, I ask whether a social planner can shift equilibrium outcomes by redesigning the information structure. Each design modifies the briefing generator's slider functions near the theoretical cutoff θ* while agents' decision process remains unchanged.

The stability design increases ambiguity near the cutoff, suppressing coordination: mean joining falls and the regime-fall rate drops. The public signal produces the largest reduction via the strategic complementarity identified by Morris & Shin (2002): agents overweight public signals because they predict others' beliefs, crowding out private information.

Upper censorship—pooling weak-regime states into a neutral signal—creates a flat “plateau” in join rates: when agents cannot distinguish θ = 0.20 from θ = 0.50, they behave as if the regime is borderline rather than clearly weak. Lower censorship produces a striking reversal: the comparative statics flip sign. When censorship is made common knowledge, its effect largely neutralizes—consistent with theory assuming informed receivers.

Section VII

The Belief–Action Wedge

Result — Surveillance Chilling Effect

Surveillance reduces mean join rates by ~13.5 percentage points across five architectures while second-order beliefs shift by less than 1 pp. Agents change what they do without changing what they believe.

In the surveillance treatment, the communication prompt warns that messages are being monitored by regime security services. The manipulation affects only the communication phase; the decision prompt is unchanged. Any difference in join rates is most parsimoniously explained by agents self-censoring their messages—contaminating the information environment with sanitized content.

The Indirect Channel

Surveillance shifts stated beliefs by only ~1 pp (not significant) while shifting join rates by ~13.5 pp (p < 0.001). Virtually the entire effect passes through the belief–action wedge—agents change behavior without changing beliefs. The R² of message content on true θ drops from 0.12 to 0.02—an 80% reduction in informational content.

The Strategic Update Gap

Second-order beliefs are unchanged by surveillance, revealing the mechanism: each agent self-censors while interpreting others' silence as genuine. Common knowledge of the chilling effect never forms. The suppressed equilibrium is sustained not because agents believe differently, but because the communication channel has been poisoned.

Among agents who privately decide to JOIN, only 19% send action-signaling messages under surveillance vs. 46% under regular communication. The word “collapse” drops from 11.6% to 0.6% of messages.

Two placebo variants—“monitored for research” (no consequences) and “aggregated anonymously”—produce no significant deviation from baseline, confirming that the chilling effect is specific to the surveillance warning's strategic content.

Section VIII

Propaganda Saturates Quickly

Following Edmond (2013), I introduce propaganda as regime plants into the communication network—agents who always STAY and inject regime-loyal messages. The overall join rate falls linearly with the number of plants (mechanical dilution), but the behavioral effect on real citizens saturates: doubling plants from 5 to 10 produces no additional effect.

Message content reveals the mechanism. Propaganda injects regime-loyal vocabulary (“loyal” rises from 1.5% to 11.4% at m = 10) while coordination language declines (“ready” from 30.5% to 18.5%). Among real agents who STAY, caution-coded messages rise from 24.2% to 38.2%—agents echo the propaganda. Among those who JOIN, action signaling remains stable at ~86%.

Instrument Interactions

Surveillance and censorship interact super-additively: censorship degrades private signal quality, making the communication channel relatively more important, and surveillance poisons that channel. With both instruments active, agents have neither private signals to trust nor authentic messages to learn from.

Conclusion

LLM agents implement threshold policies closely consistent with the unique Bayes-Nash Equilibrium across seven architectures, and this behavioral regularity makes them a viable platform for studying how information structures shape coordination outcomes.

The information environment is the key policy lever: signal design shifts posterior beliefs and therefore equilibrium thresholds, unifying persuasion, censorship, and surveillance into a single formal object. Surveillance compounds information friction through a belief–action wedge—agents maintain beliefs but suppress behavior, poisoning the communication channel.

The strategic update gap ensures this suppressed equilibrium is sustained, because common knowledge of the chilling effect never forms.

Whether the underlying mechanism is approximate Bayesian reasoning or a learned heuristic remains open. The relevant finding is that the structural fit is robust enough to serve as a reusable template for linking global-game theory, Bayesian persuasion, and empirical evidence in settings that have traditionally been out of reach for conventional experiments.
