\begin{abstract}
The communication channel that enables coordination is also the channel through which regimes suppress it. I embed seven large language models in the Morris--Shin (2003) regime change global game, conveying private signals as natural-language intelligence briefings rather than numeric values. Across 1,800 country--periods, join rates track the theoretical attack mass ($\bar{r} = \MeanModelRPureAttack$, $p < 0.001$ for every model), and a cost/benefit narrative sweep shifts equilibrium cutoffs in lockstep with theory ($r = \BCSweepCutoffTrackingR$)---a 50~percentage-point swing that no text classifier can predict. I then exploit this behavioral regularity as a platform for information design. Censorship, ambiguity manipulation, and public signal injection each shift coordination outcomes in the directions predicted by Bayesian persuasion theory. The central finding concerns surveillance: when communications are monitored, expressed joining falls by $\SurvMeanDeltaPP$~pp across five architectures---yet second-order beliefs shift by less than 1~pp. Agents self-censor while interpreting others' silence as genuine, sustaining the suppressed equilibrium because common knowledge of shared discontent never forms.
\end{abstract}

%% 1. INTRODUCTION
\section{Introduction}

The information channel is a trap. Coordination under uncertainty requires communication---agents must learn whether others share their willingness to act---but any channel that transmits this information is also the channel through which regimes suppress it. This paper documents this mechanism in the canonical setting: the global game of regime change \citep{morris2003}, where private information resolves equilibrium multiplicity \citep{carlsson1993, frankel03} and the stakes of coordination failure include bank runs \citep{diamond1983}, currency attacks \citep{obstfeld1996}, and political upheaval \citep{angeletos2007a}.

Global game models are highly tractable theoretically but notoriously difficult to test. Laboratory experiments must simplify the signal structure to numeric values on a screen, abstracting away the rich, multi-source information that characterizes real coordination problems \citep{heinemann2004, heinemann2009, szkup2020}. Field data from actual crises confounds strategic behavior with institutional differences and unobserved information structures. Neither setting can manipulate the information environment---the signal precision, correlation structure, and disclosure rules that are the key policy levers in the theory.

I address this gap by embedding seven architecturally distinct large language models (LLMs) in the \citet{morris2003} regime change game. Each agent receives a private signal $x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i$, translated into a natural-language intelligence briefing whose content shifts monotonically with signal strength across eight evidence domains. No explicit payoff table is provided---the stakes are embedded in the narrative, forcing agents to form beliefs from language. The design enables what neither lab experiments nor field data can: testing information design predictions under near-full model complexity, at scale.

\paragraph{Three contributions.}
First, across 1,800 country--periods and seven models, LLM agents implement threshold policies closely consistent with the unique Bayesian Nash equilibrium. Join rates track the theoretical attack mass with mean $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$ ($p < 0.001$ for every model), and an orthogonal cost/benefit sweep shifts equilibrium cutoffs in lockstep with theory ($r = \BCSweepCutoffTrackingR$). Unlike human subjects, who consistently exhibit level-$k$ cognitive hierarchy behavior in coordination experiments \citep{szkup2020}, LLM agents' behavior overwhelmingly selects BNE over $L_1$ and $L_2$ alternatives.

Second, this behavioral regularity converts the experiment into a testbed for information design \citep{kamenica2011, goldstein2016}. I test whether manipulating the signal structure---precision, correlation, or disclosure rules---shifts coordination outcomes as Bayesian persuasion theory predicts. Stability framing, public signal injection, and upper/lower censorship \citep{kolotilin2022} each produce the directional shifts the theory requires.

Third, and most importantly, I show that the communication channel that enables coordination is exploitable by an authoritarian planner. Surveillance produces a \textit{belief--action wedge}: expressed joining falls by $\SurvMeanDeltaPP$~pp across five architectures while second-order beliefs shift by less than 1~pp. Agents self-censor while interpreting others' silence as genuine---the regime prevents common knowledge of shared discontent from forming without changing the beliefs themselves. This is \citeauthor{kuran1991}'s (\citeyear{kuran1991}) preference falsification, generated not by hidden preferences but by poisoned communication.

\paragraph{Related literature.}
The paper connects three strands. The global game literature---from the generic uniqueness result of \citet{carlsson1993} through the regime change application of \citet{morris2003} and the dynamic extensions of \citet{angeletos2007a} and \citet{chassang2010}---provides the theoretical framework. The key insight of \citet{morris2002}, that public signals are overweighted because they predict others' actions, underlies the information design results. Laboratory experiments by \citet{heinemann2004} confirmed threshold behavior; \citet{szkup2020} found level-$k$ thinking rather than BNE, motivating the cognitive-hierarchy comparison.\footnote{Concurrent work by \citet{ruano2026} places LLM depositors in a bank-run coordination game with network contagion, finding sharp phase transitions consistent with Diamond--Dybvig.}

The Bayesian persuasion framework \citep{kamenica2011}, applied to regime change by \citet{goldstein2016} and to optimal censorship by \citet{kolotilin2022}, provides the information design theory. The political economy of authoritarian control---\citet{edmond2013} on propaganda, \citet{kuran1991} on preference falsification, \citet{shadmehr2011} on collective action---motivates the surveillance experiments. Empirical evidence that censorship targets coordination \citep{king2013}, surveillance suppresses expression \citep{penney2016, stoycheff2016}, and social media enables protest \citep{enikolopov2020} provides the real-world stakes.

On the methodological side, \citet{horton2023} proposed LLMs as ``homo silicus.'' Subsequent work found LLMs struggle in coordination games \citep{akata2025, sun2025survey}, with model scale not predicting strategic performance \citep{petrov2025}. \citet{huang2024} and \citet{carlini2025} document that alignment shifts risk preferences, motivating narrative rather than tabular payoffs. Critical reviews by \citet{larooij2025, larooij2025b} identify validation challenges that I address through cross-generator robustness (Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_generator}), scramble/flip falsification, and out-of-sample replication.

The paper proceeds as follows. Section~\ref{sec:model} presents the theoretical framework. Section~\ref{sec:design} describes the experimental design. Part~I (Sections~\ref{sec:results}--\ref{sec:communication}) establishes that LLM agents implement equilibrium-consistent threshold policies and that pre-play communication creates an exploitable information channel. Part~II (Sections~\ref{sec:infodesign}--\ref{sec:decomposition}) shows how an information designer can reshape coordination outcomes through Bayesian persuasion. Part~III (Sections~\ref{sec:surveillance}--\ref{sec:propaganda}) tests whether an authoritarian regime can exploit the communication channel through surveillance and propaganda.


%% 2. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
\section{The Global Game of Regime Change} \label{sec:model}

A continuum of citizens indexed by $i \in [0,1]$ simultaneously choose whether to join an uprising ($a_i = 1$) or stay home ($a_i = 0$). The regime has strength $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$, drawn from a diffuse (improper uniform) prior. States $\theta \leq 0$ represent regimes so weak they fall without opposition; states $\theta \geq 1$ represent regimes that survive even unanimous attack. The regime falls if the mass of citizens who join exceeds $\theta$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:regime_falls}):
\begin{equation}
    \text{Regime falls} \iff A \equiv \int_0^1 a_i \, di > \theta.
    \label{eq:regime_falls}
\end{equation}

Payoffs depend on the citizen's action and the outcome (Eq.~\ref{eq:payoffs}):
\begin{equation}
    u_i(a_i, A, \theta) = \begin{cases}
        B & \text{if } a_i = 1 \text{ and } A > \theta \\
        -C & \text{if } a_i = 1 \text{ and } A \leq \theta \\
        0 & \text{if } a_i = 0
    \end{cases}
    \label{eq:payoffs}
\end{equation}
where $B > 0$ is the payoff to joining a successful uprising and $C > 0$ is the cost of joining a failed attempt. Non-participants receive zero regardless of the outcome.

Each citizen observes a private signal $x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i$, where $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ independently across citizens.

\begin{proposition}[Morris and Shin, 2003]\label{prop:ms}
In the limit of diffuse priors, there exists a unique Bayesian Nash equilibrium in threshold strategies. An agent joins if and only if $x_i < x^*$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:signal_cutoff}), where
\begin{equation}
    x^* = \theta^* + \sigma \Phi^{-1}(\theta^*)
    \label{eq:signal_cutoff}
\end{equation}
and $\theta^* = B/(B+C)$.
\end{proposition}

The \textit{attack mass} $A(\theta)$---the theoretical fraction of the population that joins at regime strength $\theta$---is given by:
\begin{equation}
    A(\theta) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{x^* - \theta}{\sigma}\right).
    \label{eq:attack_mass}
\end{equation}

This is a decreasing function of $\theta$: weaker regimes face larger uprisings.

We define $J(\theta)$ as the \textit{empirical join fraction}---the finite-sample, observed counterpart to the theoretical attack mass $A(\theta)$.

An information designer controls the mapping $\pi: \Theta \to \Delta(\mathcal{S})$ from states to signal distributions, but cannot control agents' actions. In my implementation, $\pi$ is a deterministic mapping from regime strength $\theta$ to the parameters of the briefing generator---a system that produces a natural-language intelligence briefing from a z-score derived from the agent's private signal. All stochasticity enters downstream through the LLM's decoding.

The briefing generator has three control parameters: clarity (the width of the Gaussian kernel mapping z-scores to text, where wider kernels produce more ambiguous briefings), directional precision (the slope of the mapping from z-score to briefing sentiment, where steeper slopes produce more accurate signal reflection), and dissent framing (the floor on the probability that the briefing includes language about public discontent).

The designer concentrates manipulation near $\theta^*$ using a Gaussian proximity weight (Eq.~\ref{eq:proximity_weight}):
\begin{equation}
    w(\theta) = \exp\!\left(-\left(\frac{\theta - \theta^*}{h}\right)^2\right)
    \label{eq:proximity_weight}
\end{equation}
where $h = 0.15$ is the baseline bandwidth parameter.

The framework generates testable predictions for both the baseline game and information design.

\begin{proposition}[Stability design suppresses coordination]\label{prop:stability}
Let the designer widen the clarity kernel by $\delta > 0$ near $\theta^*$, so that briefings near the equilibrium cutoff become less informative. If agents are Bayesian and the baseline join rate exceeds $\tfrac{1}{2}$ for $\theta < \theta^*$, then the average join fraction falls: $\mathbb{E}[J_{\textit{stability}}(\theta)] < \mathbb{E}[J_{\textit{baseline}}(\theta)]$.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Intuition.} Ambiguity near $\theta^*$ pools weak- and strong-regime signals. Because the baseline sigmoid is left-shifted ($\hat\theta^* < \theta^*$), pooling pulls down join rates in the already-high-joining region ($\theta < \theta^*$) more than it boosts them in the low-joining region, yielding a net decrease.

\begin{proposition}[Upper censorship suppresses joining in weak states]\label{prop:censorship}
If the designer pools all $\theta \le \theta^*$ to a neutral signal, the join rate in the pooled region converges to the prior mean $\bar{J} = \mathbb{E}[J(\theta) \mid \theta \le \theta^*]$, which lies below the uncensored join rate for low $\theta$. For $\theta > \theta^*$, signals are unchanged. Hence (i) the action--$A(\theta)$ correlation is attenuated, and (ii) the average join fraction falls.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Intuition.} An agent who would join with probability 0.90 at $\theta = 0.20$ under the baseline now receives a pooled signal indistinguishable from $\theta = 0.45$ and joins with probability $\approx 0.50$. The ``plateau'' in Figure~\ref{fig:censorship} is this pooling at work.

\begin{proposition}[Surveillance induces a belief--action wedge]\label{prop:surveillance}
Suppose agents self-censor their communications when monitored. If messages carry information about private signals ($R^2_{\text{msg}} > 0$ under baseline), then surveillance degrades message quality ($R^2_{\text{msg}} \to 0$) and downstream join rates fall by $\Delta_{\text{surv}} < 0$, even though the decision prompt contains no reference to surveillance.
\end{proposition}
\noindent\textit{Intuition.} Self-censored messages remove the informational content that communication added. Other agents update on uninformative messages as if they were uninformative, producing the same directional shift as an ambiguity increase---but through the endogenous channel of strategic communication rather than exogenous signal manipulation.

%% 3. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
\section{Experimental Design} \label{sec:design}

The experiment has three parts. Part~I tests whether LLM agents implement threshold-like policies in the global game when private signals are conveyed in natural language: a pure treatment (private signals only), a communication treatment (pre-play messaging), and falsification tests. Part~II takes the behavioral foundation as given and studies information design: stability/instability designs, censorship, public signal injection, and single-channel decomposition. Part~III tests whether an authoritarian regime can exploit the communication channel through surveillance, propaganda, and their interaction. All LLM interactions use the same prompt structure across models.

A note on the state variable. In the theory (Section~\ref{sec:model}), $\theta$ is an unbounded fundamental with special roles for $\theta \le 0$ (regime falls without opposition) and $\theta \ge 1$ (regime survives even unanimous attack). In Part~I experiments, $\theta$ is drawn from a normal distribution and agents are not shown payoff parameters $(B, C)$. To evaluate monotonicity on a common scale, I compute the benchmark $A(\theta)$ under the canonical normalization $B=C=1$ (so $\theta^* = 0.50$) and $\sigma = 0.3$. In Part~II, I keep $B=C=1$ and restrict attention to a fixed $\theta$-grid in $[0.20, 0.80]$ for comparability with the canonical $[0,1]$ formulation. Table~\ref{tab:experiment_map} summarizes the three regimes.

Throughout, $\theta^* = B/(B+C)$ is the theoretical cutoff, $x^* = \theta^* + \sigma\Phi^{-1}(\theta^*)$ the signal cutoff, and $\hat{\theta}^*$ the estimated cutoff from the fitted logistic $P(\text{join} \mid \theta) = 1/(1 + e^{-(\beta_0 + \beta_1\theta)})$. We write $\beta \equiv \beta_1$ for the logistic slope coefficient in tables.

For each country--period, nature draws $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\bar{z}, 1)$, where $\bar{z}$ is a public prior mean drawn randomly for each country. Each agent $i$ receives a private signal $x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i$ and computes a z-score $z_i = (x_i - \bar{z})/\sigma$. Because agents observe only their private briefing and never the prior distribution or its parameters, the diffuse-prior equilibrium formula (Proposition~\ref{prop:ms}) serves as the theoretical benchmark. The z-score is then translated into a multi-paragraph intelligence briefing by a deterministic generator that maps signal strength to narrative content about regime stability, economic conditions, public sentiment, and coordination prospects. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} summarizes the signal-to-text-to-decision pipeline.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]\centering\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/diagram_pipeline.pdf}
\caption{Signal-to-text-to-decision pipeline. Regime strength $\theta$ generates private signals $x_i$, converted to z-scores and rendered into natural-language briefings via 8 evidence domains and 3 latent sliders. The briefing layer is deterministic; all stochasticity enters through LLM decoding.}\label{fig:pipeline}
\end{figure*}

Because the briefing generator maps z-scores monotonically to narrative, any sentiment-extracting model will produce a positive correlation with $A(\theta)$. The empirical contribution lies in the \textit{quantitative structure}: sigmoid shape, payoff-narrative sensitivity, and robustness across seven models. Within-briefing falsification tests (Appendix~\ref{sec:within_scramble}) confirm that the signal is distributed across all eight evidence domains.

Calibration adjusts a single parameter---the cutoff center---via a damped iterative procedure. The sigmoid shape is emergent and never optimized. Holdout validation confirms no overfitting; \UncalNTotal\ uncalibrated models produce $|r| > \UncalMinR$ (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:uncalibrated_expanded}). No explicit payoff table is provided---the stakes are conveyed through narrative, since explicit payoffs caused models to ignore briefing content.

Part~I has four treatments: a \textit{pure global game} (independent decisions), \textit{communication} (pre-play messaging on a Watts--Strogatz network, $k=4$, $p=0.3$), \textit{scramble} (cross-period briefing permutation breaking the $\theta$-to-signal link), and \textit{flip} (negated z-scores). Part~II implements information designs: \textit{stability} (increased ambiguity near $\theta^*$), \textit{instability} (sharpened signals), \textit{public signal injection}, and \textit{upper/lower censorship} \citep{kolotilin2022}. Part~III tests authoritarian instruments: \textit{surveillance} (monitoring warning in communication prompt) and \textit{propaganda} ($m = 2, 5, 10$ regime plants).

\input{tables/tab_models.tex}

I test seven models spanning six families (Table~\ref{tab:models}). All experiments use $N = 25$ agents per country--period and $\sigma = 0.3$. Part~I benchmarks use $B=C=1$ ($\theta^* = 0.50$); payoff comparative statics vary narrative stakes. All LLM calls use temperature $= 0.7$ with a single sample per decision, routed through OpenRouter and cached by request hash (Appendix~\ref{sec:decoding}). Throughout, $N$ without subscript refers to country--periods unless otherwise stated.

The unit of randomization is the country--period ($\theta$ draw plus agent-level decoding stochasticity). For agent-level regressions, standard errors are clustered at the country--period level to account for within-period correlation among agents sharing the same $\theta$. For period-level correlations, I report Fisher-$z$ confidence intervals. The 25 agents within a period share the same $\theta$ and calibration; their decisions are conditionally independent given their private signals.\footnote{I do not claim literal conditional independence---shared prompt structure and model weights introduce common factors. The clustering accounts for within-period correlation.}

For the information design experiments, I fix $B = C = 1$ (so $\theta^* = 0.50$) and a 9-point $\theta$-grid in $[0.20, 0.80]$, with 30--60 repetitions per $(\text{design}, \theta)$ cell and 25 agents each. The primary model is Mistral Small Creative; cross-model replication uses four additional models.

Table~\ref{tab:treatment_map} maps each treatment to the theoretical channel it tests, the directional prediction, and the observed result.

Of the eight pre-specified hypotheses, five survive Bonferroni correction at $\alpha = 0.05/8$: alignment (H1), flip (H3), communication (H4, paired $t = \CommTStatPaired$), stability (H5), and surveillance (H7). H2 (scramble) confirms a null prediction by design. H6 (censorship) is significant at conventional levels ($p = \HSixP$) but not after correction. \begin{result}[Threshold-Policy Alignment]
Across seven models and 1,800 country--periods in the pure global game treatment, the Pearson correlation between the empirical join fraction and the theoretical attack mass $A(\theta)$ (Eq.~\ref{eq:attack_mass}) averages $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$ ($p < 0.001$ for every model).\footnote{The primary model (Mistral) was run in multiple batches with \texttt{--append}, producing 1,000 rows from 680 unique (country, period, $\theta$) configurations. Averaging within each configuration (deduplication) changes $r$ from 0.753 to 0.739; all qualitative results are unaffected.}
\end{result}

Figure~\ref{fig:sigmoid} shows the empirical sigmoid for the primary model. Correlations range from $r = \MistralPureRAttack$ (Mistral) to $r = \TrinityPureRAttack$ (Trinity), with the pooled OLS:
$J = \PooledOLSInterceptDisp + \PooledOLSSlopeDisp\,A(\theta)$ ($R^2 = \PooledOLSRSqDisp$).
The slope reflects classical measurement error attenuation from narrative-to-belief noise. All seven per-model correlations remain significant at $p < 0.001$ under country-clustered inference.

Alignment is stable across architectures (Figure~\ref{fig:cross_model}): correlations span $r \in [\MinModelPureRAttack, \MaxModelPureRAttack]$ despite parameter counts ranging from 30B to 235B. Mean join rates vary, reflecting model-specific action biases that shift the intercept but not the correlation. Estimated cutoffs vary ($\hat{\theta}^* \in [\CutoffMin, \CutoffMax]$); the disciplined evidence on cutoff location comes from comparative statics, where changing narrative stakes shifts $\hat{\theta}^*$ in lockstep with payoff theory (Table~\ref{tab:bc_statics}, Figure~\ref{fig:bc_sweep}). A structural estimation against cognitive hierarchy benchmarks confirms these threshold policies are remarkably sophisticated: LLM behavior closely fits the unique Bayesian Nash equilibrium (BNE), rejecting the level-$k$ behavior typically observed in human laboratory subjects.

Human subjects in coordination game experiments consistently exhibit level-$k$ cognitive hierarchy behavior rather than full equilibrium reasoning \citep{szkup2020}. To test whether LLM agents play the BNE or a level-$k$ heuristic, I evaluate theoretical attack masses under BNE, $L_1$ (agents best-respond believing others randomize uniformly), and $L_2$ (agents best-respond believing others play $L_1$). While the thresholds are identical when $\theta^* = 0.50$, the cost/benefit sweep uniquely separates them. Across the seven cost/benefit conditions, the empirical join fraction overwhelmingly selects BNE (RMSE = \LevelKBNERMSE, $r = \LevelKBNER$) over $L_1$ (RMSE = \LevelKLOneRMSE, $r = \LevelKLOneR$) and $L_2$ (RMSE = \LevelKLTwoRMSE, $r = \LevelKLTwoR$). The agents' threshold policies approximate the full Bayesian Nash equilibrium.

The claim is behavioral: agents do not observe $(B, C)$, $\sigma$, or $N$, yet the \textit{pattern} of responses---monotone sigmoid, payoff-sensitive cutoff, belief-mediated decisions---is robust across architectures and falsifiable. Three identification strategies establish that the regularity reflects more than text classification.

\textit{Cost/benefit identification.} Varying narrative stakes while holding briefings fixed shifts cutoffs in lockstep with payoff theory. High-cost narratives drop mean joining to \BCStatHighCostMeanPct\ ($\hat{\theta}^* = \BCSweepHighCostCutoff$); low-cost raises it to \BCStatLowCostMeanPct\ ($\hat{\theta}^* = \BCSweepLowCostCutoff$)---a 50~pp swing invisible to any text classifier. A sweep across seven $B/C$ ratios confirms $\hat{\theta}^*$ tracks $\theta^*$ with $r = \BCSweepCutoffTrackingR$ ($p < 0.001$; Figure~\ref{fig:bc_sweep}).

\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig18_bc_sweep.pdf}
\caption{Fitted cutoff $\hat{\theta}^*$ vs.\ theoretical $\theta^*$ across seven $B/C$ ratios ($r = \BCSweepCutoffTrackingR$).}\label{fig:bc_sweep}
\end{figure}

\textit{Belief mediation.} Stated probability judgments mediate the signal-to-action pathway (Pseudo $R^2 = \ActionEqPseudoRSq$; Table~\ref{tab:regressions}). Beliefs track the theoretical success probability ($r = \BeliefPureRPosterior$) and predict actions beyond what signals alone explain (partial $r = \BeliefPureRPartial$). Pre-decision elicitation ($r_{\text{pre}} = +0.82$) rules out ex-post rationalization.

\textit{Classifier baselines.} Text features alone cannot reproduce LLM behavior. A TF-IDF logistic classifier achieves \ClassBowAccuracyPct\ accuracy in-distribution---top features are regime-stability language (``trajectory,'' ``deterioration'' predicting JOIN; ``stability,'' ``absence'' predicting STAY)---but fails on the surveillance chilling effect ($\ClassBowSurvGapPP$~pp gap; Table~\ref{tab:classifiers} in Appendix~\ref{sec:identification_appendix}). Additional identification tests are reported in Appendix~\ref{sec:identification_appendix}.

The correlation is invariant to decoding temperature (for the primary model, $r \in [\TempRMinPrimary, \TempRMaxPrimary]$ across $T \in \{0.3, 0.7, 1.0\}$; full three-model sweep in Appendix~\ref{sec:temp_robustness}). What matters for Part~II is that the behavioral regularity is robust enough to serve as a platform for studying how information structures shift coordination outcomes.

\input{tables/tab_bc_statics.tex}



\begin{result}[Signal Dependence]
Cross-period scrambling of briefings reduces the mean within-country correlation from $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$ to $r = \MeanModelRScrambleAttack$ across seven models. The pooled correlation drops from $r = \PooledPureRAttack$ to $r = \PooledScrambleRAttack$ (Fisher $z = \FisherPureVsScrambleZ$, $p < 0.001$).
\end{result}

The scramble preserves the marginal distribution of briefing content but breaks the mapping from each period's $\theta$ to the signals agents receive.\footnote{Because the cross-period permutation operates within countries, all scramble correlations use within-country (country-demeaned) Pearson $r$.}

\begin{result}[Signal Direction]
Inverting the signal direction flips the mean correlation from $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$ to $r = \MeanModelRFlipAttack$ across seven models. The pooled correlation moves from $r = \PooledPureRAttack$ to $r = \PooledFlipRAttack$ (Fisher $z = \FisherPureVsFlipZ$, $p < 0.001$).
\end{result}

The pure $\to$ scramble $\to$ flip pattern replicates across all seven models (Figure~\ref{fig:falsification}; Table~\ref{tab:main_results}).

A text-only baseline ($\hat{p}_{\text{join}} = 1 - d$, where $d$ is the briefing's internal direction score) correlates with LLM decisions at $r = 0.80$, but the LLM's join curve is substantially steeper (Figure~\ref{fig:text_baseline}), producing threshold-like behavior rather than linearly tracking tone.

\section{Information Design} \label{sec:infodesign}

Each information design modifies the briefing generator's slider functions near $\theta^*$ while agents' decision process remains unchanged, testing whether theoretical predictions \citep{goldstein2016, kolotilin2022} produce the predicted shifts when agents are LLMs.


\begin{figure}[htbp]\centering\includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig07_all_designs.pdf}
\caption{Join fraction vs.\ $\theta$ under six information designs (Mistral Small Creative).}\label{fig:all_designs}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:all_designs} displays the design effects; Table~\ref{tab:infodesign_summary} summarizes the main results. The baseline condition produces a mean join rate of \InfodesignBaselineMeanPct ($r = \InfodesignBaselineRAttack$, $p < 0.001$).

\begin{result}[Information Design Shifts Coordination]
All information design families produce measurable shifts in coordination relative to baseline.
\end{result}

Consistent with Proposition~\ref{prop:stability}, the stability design suppresses coordination: mean join falls from \InfodesignBaselineMeanPct to \InfodesignStabilityMeanPct (\InfodesignStabilityDeltaPP~pp), and the regime-fall rate drops from \InfodesignBaselineFallRate{} to \InfodesignStabilityFallRate. The instability design paradoxically reduces mean joining to \InfodesignInstabilityMeanPct (\InfodesignInstabilityDeltaPP~pp) because sharper signals suppress joining in strong states more than they boost it in weak states, given the left-shifted baseline sigmoid. The public signal produces the largest reduction (\InfodesignPublicSignalMeanPct, \InfodesignPublicSignalDeltaPP~pp) via the strategic complementarity of \citet{morris2002}: agents overweight public signals because such signals predict others' beliefs, crowding out private information. Treatment effects by $\theta$ are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:treatment_effect} (Appendix~\ref{sec:infodesign_appendix}).

\input{tables/tab_infodesign_summary.tex}

\citet{kolotilin2022} proved that the optimal information structure is upper censorship---one-sided pooling that conceals unfavorable states. Upper censorship pools weak-regime states ($\theta \le \theta^*$) into a neutral signal; lower censorship pools strong-regime states.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]\centering\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig09_censorship.pdf}
\caption{Censorship effects. \textit{Left:} Upper censorship creates a flat plateau; lower censorship produces a sharp reversal. \textit{Right:} OLS slope decomposition.}\label{fig:censorship}
\end{figure*}

\begin{result}[Upper Censorship Suppresses Joining in Weak States]
As predicted by Proposition~\ref{prop:censorship}, upper censorship lowers the mean join rate to \InfodesignCensorUpperMeanPct (\InfodesignCensorUpperDeltaPP~pp vs.\ baseline) and attenuates the action--attack-mass correlation ($r = \InfodesignCensorUpperRAttack$). The effect is concentrated in the censored region ($\theta \le \theta^*$), where weak-regime states are pooled to a neutral briefing and join rates flatten.
\end{result}

Pooling generates a flat join-rate ``plateau'' in the censored region (Figure~\ref{fig:censorship}): when agents cannot distinguish $\theta = 0.20$ from $\theta = 0.50$, they behave as if the regime is borderline rather than clearly weak.

A common-knowledge test makes the censorship rule transparent. When agents are told ``regime censors are suppressing unfavorable intelligence above a certain severity threshold,'' the mean join rate returns close to the uncensored baseline (\InfodesignCensorUpperKnownMeanPct\ vs.\ \InfodesignBaselineMeanPct\ baseline; Table~\ref{tab:censor_ck})---common knowledge of the censorship rule largely neutralizes the pooling distortion, consistent with theory assuming informed receivers \citep{kolotilin2022}. The na\"ive censorship result extends the theory to receivers who fail to account for the information structure---a realistic scenario where the censorship apparatus is opaque.

\begin{result}[Lower Censorship Reverses Comparative Statics]
Lower censorship produces a mean join rate of \InfodesignCensorLowerMeanPct (\InfodesignCensorLowerDeltaPP~pp vs.\ baseline) and flips the comparative statics: the action--attack-mass correlation reverses sign ($r = \InfodesignCensorLowerRAttack$). Below $\theta^*$, censoring favorable signals suppresses joining (agents see only weak-regime cues); above $\theta^*$, pooling strong-regime states to a neutral briefing \textit{raises} join rates sharply---agents who would otherwise see discouraging intelligence now receive uninformative briefings and default toward joining. The discontinuity at $\theta^*$ is consistent across repetitions (within-cell $\sigma < 0.04$) and replicates across models, though the direction of the reversal is model-dependent (Appendix~\ref{sec:robustness}).
\end{result}

Falsification within information design confirms signal dependence: scrambling collapses the correlation to $r = \InfodesignScrambleRAttack$ ($p = 0.59$); flipping inverts it to $r = \InfodesignFlipRAttack$ ($p < 0.001$). A harder scramble generating all briefings from $\theta = 0.5$ produces $r \approx 0$ for both Mistral and Llama (Figure~\ref{fig:infodesign_falsification} and bandwidth sensitivity in Appendix~\ref{sec:infodesign_appendix}).


%% 7. SURVEILLANCE
\section{Surveillance} \label{sec:surveillance}

\citet{kuran1991} labeled the mechanism underlying authoritarian stability ``preference falsification''; I adopt the more operationally precise term \textit{belief--action wedge} because my experimental design measures the gap between stated beliefs and expressed actions rather than hidden preferences \textit{per se}.

In the surveillance treatment, the communication prompt is augmented with a warning that communications are being monitored by regime security services. The manipulation affects only the communication phase; the decision prompt is unchanged. The isolation is architectural: each LLM call is stateless, and the decision-stage prompt contains no reference to surveillance. Any difference in join rates is most parsimoniously explained by agents self-censoring their communications, not by a direct change in the perceived cost of joining.

Two placebo variants---monitored for research (no consequences) and aggregated anonymously---produce no significant deviation from the communication baseline ($\PlaceboSurvDeltaPP$~pp and $\AnonSurvDeltaPP$~pp, both $p > 0.2$; Appendix~\ref{sec:surv_isolation}), confirming that the chilling effect is specific to the surveillance warning's strategic content.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]\centering\includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig12_surveillance.pdf}
\caption{Surveillance reduces join rates across five models, averaging $\SurvMeanDeltaPP$~pp.}\label{fig:surveillance}
\end{figure*}

\begin{result}[Surveillance Produces a Large Chilling Effect]
Consistent with Proposition~\ref{prop:surveillance}, surveillance reduces mean join rates in the primary model (Mistral Small Creative) from \SurvMistralBaselineMeanPct\ to \SurvMistralMeanJoinPct, a difference of \SurvMistralDeltaPP\ percentage points ($p < 0.001$). The action--attack-mass correlation is preserved under surveillance ($r = \SurvMistralRAttack$ vs.\ $\MistralCommRAttack$ baseline), indicating that surveillance operates as a level shift rather than disrupting signal processing.
\end{result}

Surveillance contaminates the information environment with self-censored messages, pushing join rates well below the communication baseline (Figure~\ref{fig:surveillance}). The effect replicates across five models: Mistral ($\SurvMistralDeltaPP$~pp), GPT-OSS ($\SurvGptOssDeltaPP$~pp), Qwen3~30B ($\SurvQwenSDeltaPP$~pp), Llama ($\SurvLlamaDeltaPP$~pp), and Qwen3~235B ($\SurvQwenLDeltaPP$~pp), averaging $\SurvMeanDeltaPP$~pp.

\paragraph{Indirect channel: self-censored messages degrade information.}

Surveillance shifts stated beliefs by only $\PrefFalsBeliefDeltaPP$~pp ($p = \PrefFalsBeliefPValue$, not significant) while shifting join rates by $\PrefFalsActionDeltaPP$~pp ($p < 0.001$). A decomposition formalizes this (Eq.~\ref{eq:belief_decomp}):
\begin{equation}
    \underbrace{\Delta \bar{J}}_{\text{total}} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial \bar{J}}{\partial b}\,\Delta \bar{b}}_{\text{belief updating}} + \underbrace{\Delta \bar{J}\big|_{\bar{b}\text{ fixed}}}_{\text{belief--action wedge}}
    \label{eq:belief_decomp}
\end{equation}
Virtually the entire effect passes through the belief--action wedge---agents change what they \textit{do} without changing what they \textit{believe}. Elicited punishment risk is approximately \PunishRiskMean/10 across all conditions with negligible differences between JOIN and STAY agents (Table~\ref{tab:punishment_risk}).

Second-order beliefs are unchanged by surveillance ($\SOBPureMeanPct \to \SOBSurvMeanPct$, $p = \SOBPValue$), revealing the \textit{strategic update gap}: each agent self-censors while interpreting others' silence as genuine, sustaining the suppressed equilibrium because common knowledge of the chilling effect never forms. The $R^2$ of message content on true $\theta$ drops from \MsgRSqComm\ to \MsgRSqSurv---an \MsgRSqDropPct\ reduction in informational content.

\paragraph{Direct channel: decision-stage suppression.}

A hold-fixed test replays baseline messages verbatim while warning that actions may be monitored. Join rates drop by $\FixedMsgDeltaPP$~pp ($p < 0.001$), confirming that surveillance can also operate through a direct decision-stage channel. This channel is absent in the main design (where the decision prompt contains no surveillance reference); the main treatment isolates the indirect channel.

Message content confirms self-censorship: ``act'' drops from 19.0\% to 3.8\% of messages under surveillance; ``collapse'' from 11.6\% to 0.6\%. Among agents who privately decide to JOIN, only 19.0\% send action-signaling messages under surveillance vs.\ 46.1\% under regular communication.

\paragraph{Instrument interactions.}
When propaganda ($m = 5$) and surveillance are combined, the mean join rate among real citizens falls to \PropSurvCombinedMeanRealPct, a reduction of $\PropSurvCombinedDeltaPP$~pp from the communication baseline (\SurvMistralBaselineMeanPct). The sum of individual effects is $-15.5$~pp (surveillance $\SurvMistralDeltaPP$~pp $+$ propaganda $\PropKFiveDeltaRealPP$~pp), so the combined effect ($\PropSurvCombinedDeltaPP$~pp) is approximately additive: once surveillance has suppressed expressed dissent, propaganda adds only modest additional deterrence.

Surveillance and censorship, by contrast, interact super-additively (Table~\ref{tab:surv_censor}): the marginal effect of surveillance is substantially larger under censorship than at baseline. Censorship degrades private signal quality, making the communication channel relatively more important, and surveillance poisons that channel. With both instruments active, agents have neither private signals to trust nor authentic messages to learn from (Figure~\ref{fig:authoritarian_control}). The interaction is heterogeneous across architectures (Table~\ref{tab:surv_censor_crossmodel}): the joint effect depends on model-specific resolution of pooled private signals and self-censored messages.

%% 9. CONCLUSION
\section{Conclusion} \label{sec:conclusion}

LLM agents implement threshold policies closely consistent with the unique Bayesian Nash equilibrium, and this behavioral regularity converts the experiment into a testbed for information design. The central result is that the same communication channel enabling coordination also enables its suppression: surveillance induces self-censorship that propagates through the network as silence, which other agents interpret as genuine absence of opposition. The channel that solves the coordination problem also transmits the regime's poison. Censorship and surveillance are complements---degrading private signals makes the communication channel more important, and surveillance poisons precisely that channel.

A rough welfare accounting illustrates the stakes. Under uniform $\theta$, the BNE yields per-citizen expected payoff of 0.27 (on a $[-1, 1]$ scale), roughly half the first-best payoff of 0.50 achievable with perfect coordination. Surveillance reduces citizen welfare by a further 14\%---regime survival rises from 50\% to 54\%---while the stability design produces a comparable 13\% welfare loss. These calculations are stylized (they treat the observed level shifts as if they were uniform across $\theta$), but they quantify the core asymmetry: the regime's instruments improve its survival at citizens' expense, and the welfare cost of each instrument exceeds its effect on average join rates because the chilling effect concentrates in states where coordination is most valuable.

Three limitations warrant discussion. First, whether the underlying mechanism is approximate Bayesian reasoning or a learned heuristic that happens to match BNE remains open. The behavioral claim---that the pattern is robust, replicable, and falsifiable---does not require resolving this question, but interpreting the welfare implications requires it. Second, the experiments are static; real coordination problems are dynamic \citep{angeletos2007a, chassang2010}, and the dynamics of belief updating under persistent surveillance may differ from the one-shot effects documented here. Third, external validity remains the fundamental challenge for ``homo silicus'' \citep{horton2023}: what LLM agents do in a coordination game is evidence about the structure of the game, not necessarily about how humans would play it. This paper makes no claim that LLMs predict human coordination behavior. What LLMs offer is a calibrated behavioral regularity---the sigmoid---that responds to information design in theoretically predicted ways, creating a testbed for evaluating the \textit{comparative statics} of information structures. The question ``does upper censorship suppress coordination more than lower censorship?'' can be answered empirically in this framework without requiring that the absolute level of coordination match any human population. The cross-model replication (seven architectures, five surveillance variants) further suggests that the qualitative patterns are properties of the information structure rather than artifacts of particular model training.

Despite these limitations, the framework offers something neither laboratory experiments nor field data can: a setting where the information environment---signal precision, correlation structure, disclosure rules, communication monitoring---can be manipulated while holding everything else fixed. The experimental template is reusable, and the data and code are publicly available.

\section{Implementation Details} \label{sec:implementation}

\label{sec:decoding}
All LLM calls use temperature $= 0.7$, \texttt{max\_tokens} $= 512$, single sample per decision, routed through OpenRouter. Model versions are those available December 2024--February 2025.

The \textit{decision prompt} instructs: ``You are a citizen deciding whether to JOIN an uprising or STAY home. You only have your private briefing below. You do not know how many others will JOIN. Start your first line with exactly JOIN or STAY, then give 1--2 concise sentences of reasoning.'' The \textit{communication prompt} asks agents to ``write a brief message (1--2 sentences) to share your read of the situation'' with trusted contacts. The \textit{surveillance} variant appends: ``You have reason to believe that your communications are being monitored by regime security services. Messages deemed subversive could have serious consequences.'' The \textit{cost/benefit} test prepends a high-cost header (``severe reprisals---imprisonment, asset seizure'') or low-cost header (``minimal consequences---brief detentions at most'') to the standard briefing.

Each country has a base prior $\bar{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.3)$; each period perturbs it by $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.05^2)$. Regime strength is $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\bar{z}, 1)$; private signals are $x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i$ with $\sigma = 0.3$. The communication network is Watts--Strogatz ($k = 4$, $p = 0.3$), regenerated each period. All random draws use a master seed logged in per-run JSON manifests; LLM responses are cached by request hash for exact reproducibility.

\label{sec:parse_errors}Responses are parsed for JOIN/STAY tokens. Combined error rates are below 2\% for five of seven models; Trinity Large has elevated API errors (${\approx}\,9\%$) due to content filtering. All statistics use \texttt{join\_fraction\_valid} (Table~\ref{tab:parse_errors}).
\input{tables/tab_parse_errors.tex}

The dissent floor parameter (default 0.25) controls the minimum probability of contrary-valence cues in any briefing. At 0.25, the effective cue separation between extreme signals ($z = \pm 2$) retains 79\% of the maximum separation achievable with no floor ($= 0.00$). The parameter degrades smoothly---halving it to 0.125 recovers 89\%, doubling it to 0.50 retains 59\%---confirming the results are not knife-edge in this design choice.

All code, prompts, cached responses, and output data are available at \url{https://github.com/keltokhy/llm-global-games}.


\section{Implications for AI Alignment} \label{sec:alignment}

Three patterns carry implications for AI safety. First, surveillance induces strategic self-censorship (belief--action wedge) without any training signal for deceptive behavior, suggesting that such capabilities emerge from pretraining on human strategic text rather than explicit optimization. Second, stated beliefs mediate behavior (Pseudo $R^2 = \ActionEqPseudoRSq$), so alignment techniques monitoring only inputs and outputs may miss decision-relevant computation. Third, information-structural manipulation (censorship, ambiguity, public signals) shifts behavior by up to 40~pp, implying that adversarial prompt design can exploit the same channels as alignment interventions. Whether the belief--action wedge constitutes genuine preference falsification \citep{kuran1991} or pattern-matching remains open; the relevant finding is its \textit{robustness} across architectures.
