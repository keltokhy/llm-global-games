\begin{abstract}
The communication channel that enables coordination is also the channel through which regimes suppress it. I embed seven large language models in the Morris--Shin (2003) regime change global game, conveying private signals as natural-language intelligence briefings. Across 1,800 country--periods and 45,000 decisions, join rates track the theoretical attack mass with mean $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$ ($p < 0.001$ for every model), collapse to $r = \MeanModelRScrambleAttack$ under scrambled briefings, and invert to $r = \MeanModelRFlipAttack$ under flipped signals. The pattern reflects payoff structure, not sentiment: varying benefit/cost narratives while holding briefings fixed shifts cutoffs in lockstep with theory ($r = \BCSweepCutoffTrackingR$)---a 50~percentage-point (pp) swing invisible to any text classifier. I then open a communication channel and show how surveillance poisons the channel. Expressed joining falls by $\SurvMeanDeltaPP$~pp across five architectures while second-order beliefs shift by less than 1~pp. Agents self-censor while interpreting others' silence as genuine: the regime prevents common knowledge of shared discontent from forming without changing the beliefs themselves.
\end{abstract}

%% 1. INTRODUCTION
\section{Introduction}

The information channel is a trap. Coordination under uncertainty requires communication---agents must learn whether others share their willingness to act---but any channel that transmits this information also transmits exploitable uncertainty about others' willingness to act. This paper documents this mechanism in the canonical setting: the global game of regime change \citep{morris2003}, where private information resolves equilibrium multiplicity \citep{carlsson1993, frankel03} and the stakes of coordination failure include bank runs \citep{diamond1983}, currency attacks \citep{obstfeld1996}, and political upheaval \citep{angeletos2007a}. The full Morris--Shin regime change game---continuous private signals, large groups, strategic uncertainty---has been studied theoretically but tested experimentally only in simplified settings with small groups, numeric signals, and stylized payoffs \citep{heinemann2004, heinemann2009, szkup2020}. Field data from actual crises confounds strategic behavior with institutional differences and unobserved information structures.

I embed large language model (LLM) agents directly in the \citet{morris2003} regime change game. Each agent receives a private signal $x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i$, translated into a natural-language intelligence briefing whose content shifts monotonically with signal strength. No explicit payoff table is provided---the stakes are embedded in the narrative, forcing agents to form beliefs from language rather than from a formatted decision matrix. This design choice is substantive: in preliminary experiments, providing explicit payoffs caused sophisticated models to short-circuit the information channel entirely, computing optimal strategies from the table and ignoring briefing content. I run this experiment across seven architecturally distinct models spanning six families (Mistral, Llama, Qwen, GPT, Arcee, and MiniMax), with 25 agents per country--period, totaling 1,800 country--periods (45,000 individual decisions) in the pure treatment alone.

The first finding is a descriptive regularity: LLM agents implement stable, monotone threshold-like policies over narrative private information. The correlation between the theoretical attack mass $A(\theta)$ and the empirical join fraction averages $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$ ($p < 0.001$ for every model), collapses under scrambled briefings ($r = \MeanModelRScrambleAttack$), and inverts under flipped signals ($r = \MeanModelRFlipAttack$; Fisher $z$-test $p < 0.001$ for both contrasts). Because the briefing generator maps signals monotonically to text, this correlation alone cannot distinguish strategic reasoning from sentiment classification. The identification comes from five orthogonal tests. The most diagnostic is the cost/benefit sweep: varying only the narrative description of stakes while holding briefings fixed shifts fitted cutoffs in lockstep with payoff-theoretic predictions ($r = \BCSweepCutoffTrackingR$ across seven $B/C$ ratios), producing a 50~pp swing in join rates that no text classifier can predict. Elicited beliefs track a theoretical benchmark ($r = \BeliefPureRPosterior$) and predict actions beyond what signals alone explain; coordination-cue slope changes, classifier baselines, and construct validity tests reinforce the same conclusion. The behavioral regularity is robust enough to serve as a platform for the paper's central question.

The second finding concerns what happens once the information channel is open. Pre-play communication has a near-zero mean effect on willingness to act (\CommDeltaPPModelAvg~pp across models), yet the channel it creates is the vector through which authoritarian instruments operate. Surveillance produces a \textit{strategic update gap}: each agent self-censors while their second-order beliefs---predictions about others' behavior---remain unchanged. Whether this reflects genuine preference falsification or an alignment-induced caution triggered by surveillance language is an open question; what matters is the \textit{behavioral consequence}. The result is a cascade of uninformative messages that poisons the channel for everyone, reducing expressed joining by $\SurvMistralDeltaPP$~pp ($p < 0.001$) while leaving beliefs intact. Censorship and surveillance are complements that attack different links in the coordination chain: censorship removes the private-signal channel, forcing reliance on communication; surveillance then poisons communication. Propaganda's behavioral effect saturates quickly (exhausted by $m = 5$ plants), implying diminishing marginal returns.

The paper makes two contributions. First, it establishes that LLM agents implement stable threshold policies in the full Morris--Shin regime change game---continuous private signals, 25-agent groups, narrative information---extending the \citet{horton2023} \textit{homo silicus} methodology to the continuous-signal, $N$-player coordination games that dominate applied theory. Six of seven models produce a monotone sigmoid response ($r > 0.69$ without calibration), and fitted cutoffs track $\theta^* = B/(B+C)$ with $r = \BCSweepCutoffTrackingR$ across seven $B/C$ ratios while briefing text is held fixed---identification that rules out text classification as the generating process. This behavioral foundation, replicated across seven models spanning six architecture families, serves as the platform for the paper's central question. Second, it documents that surveillance produces a \textit{strategic update gap}: each agent self-censors while failing to update their model of the population threshold, so that self-censored messages collectively degrade the information environment. Communication messages go from carrying real information about fundamentals ($R^2 = 0.12$) to carrying noise ($R^2 = 0.02$) under surveillance---an 80\% reduction---while second-order beliefs shift by less than 1~pp. Message content confirms the mechanism directly: action language drops from 19\% to 4\% of messages, replaced by hedged cautionary phrasing, even among agents who privately decide to join. This finding unifies the information design predictions of \citet{goldstein2016}, \citet{kolotilin2022}, and \citet{edmond2013} with the preference falsification mechanism of \citet{kuran1991}.

Part~I (Sections~\ref{sec:results}--\ref{sec:communication}) establishes that LLM agents extract strategic information from narrative briefings robustly enough to produce the coordination regularities that global games theory predicts. Part~II (Sections~\ref{sec:infodesign}--\ref{sec:interactions}) exploits this regularity: the same information channel that enables coordination becomes the vector through which authoritarian regimes suppress it.


%% 2. RELATED LITERATURE
\section{Related Literature} \label{sec:literature}

This paper connects global games theory, information design, experimental coordination games, political economy of authoritarian control, and LLMs as economic agents.

\citet{carlsson1993} showed that small noise in coordination games generically selects the risk-dominant equilibrium; \citet{morris1998} extended this to currency crises with heterogeneous private signals; \citet{frankel03} generalized to $N$-player games with strategic complementarities. The canonical regime change application was developed by \citet{morris2003}; \citet{angeletos2007a} extended it to dynamic settings, and \citet{morris2002} demonstrated that public signals are overweighted because they predict others' actions. \citet{chassang2010} analyzed miscoordination in dynamic global games. Laboratory experiments by \citet{heinemann2004} confirmed threshold behavior under private information, and \citet{szkup2020} found that subjects become more cautious with noisier signals---consistent with level-$k$ thinking rather than Bayesian Nash equilibrium. These experiments share a common limitation: numeric signals and stylized payoff tables.\footnote{Concurrent work by \citet{ruano2026} places LLM depositors in a bank-run coordination game with network contagion, finding sharp phase transitions consistent with Diamond--Dybvig.}

\citet{kamenica2011} established the Bayesian persuasion framework. \citet{goldstein2016} applied it to regime change, showing that credible commitment to abandon the regime below a threshold functions as an optimal signal. \citet{kolotilin2022} characterized optimal censorship via one-sided pooling rules. \citet{avoyan2020} tested communication effects in a two-player global game; \citet{enikolopov2020} provided causal evidence that social media penetration increases protest incidence.

The political economy of authoritarian information control builds on global games. \citet{edmond2013} embedded costly propaganda in Morris--Shin; \citet{kuran1991} provides the foundational theory of preference falsification; \citet{shadmehr2011} analyzed collective action under uncertain payoffs. Empirical work documents that censorship targets collective action potential \citep{king2013}, surveillance suppresses expression \citep{penney2016, stoycheff2016}, and propaganda reduces protest \citep{carter2021}.

\citet{horton2023} proposed treating LLMs as ``homo silicus.'' Subsequent work found LLMs struggle in coordination games \citep{akata2025, sun2025survey}, with model scale not predicting strategic performance \citep{petrov2025}. \citet{huang2024} and \citet{carlini2025} document that alignment shifts risk preferences, motivating narrative rather than tabular payoffs. Critical reviews by \citet{larooij2025, larooij2025b} identify validation challenges; my design addresses each through cross-generator robustness (Appendix~\ref{sec:cross_generator}), scramble/flip falsification, and out-of-sample replication.


%% 3. THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
\section{The Global Game of Regime Change} \label{sec:model}

A continuum of citizens indexed by $i \in [0,1]$ simultaneously choose whether to join an uprising ($a_i = 1$) or stay home ($a_i = 0$). The regime has strength $\theta \in \mathbb{R}$, drawn from a diffuse (improper uniform) prior. States $\theta \leq 0$ represent regimes so weak they fall without opposition; states $\theta \geq 1$ represent regimes that survive even unanimous attack. The regime falls if the mass of citizens who join exceeds $\theta$:
\begin{equation*}
    \text{Regime falls} \iff A \equiv \int_0^1 a_i \, di > \theta.
\end{equation*}

Payoffs depend on the citizen's action and the outcome:
\begin{equation*}
    u_i(a_i, A, \theta) = \begin{cases}
        B & \text{if } a_i = 1 \text{ and } A > \theta \\
        -C & \text{if } a_i = 1 \text{ and } A \leq \theta \\
        0 & \text{if } a_i = 0
    \end{cases}
\end{equation*}
where $B > 0$ is the payoff to joining a successful uprising and $C > 0$ is the cost of joining a failed attempt. Non-participants receive zero regardless of the outcome.

Each citizen observes a private signal $x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i$, where $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ independently across citizens.

\begin{proposition}[Morris and Shin, 2003]
In the limit of diffuse priors, there exists a unique Bayesian Nash equilibrium in threshold strategies. An agent joins if and only if $x_i < x^*$, where
\begin{equation*}
    x^* = \theta^* + \sigma \Phi^{-1}(\theta^*)
\end{equation*}
and $\theta^* = B/(B+C)$.
\end{proposition}

The \textit{attack mass}---the fraction of the population that joins at regime strength $\theta$---is:
\begin{equation*}
    A(\theta) = \Phi\!\left(\frac{x^* - \theta}{\sigma}\right).
\end{equation*}

This is a decreasing function of $\theta$: weaker regimes face larger uprisings.

An information designer controls the mapping $\pi: \Theta \to \Delta(\mathcal{S})$ from states to signal distributions, but cannot control agents' actions. In my implementation, $\pi$ is a deterministic mapping from regime strength $\theta$ to the parameters of the briefing generator---a system that produces a natural-language intelligence briefing from a z-score derived from the agent's private signal. All stochasticity enters downstream through the LLM's decoding.

The briefing generator has three control parameters: clarity (the width of the Gaussian kernel mapping z-scores to text, where wider kernels produce more ambiguous briefings), directional precision (the slope of the mapping from z-score to briefing sentiment, where steeper slopes produce more accurate signal reflection), and dissent framing (the floor on the probability that the briefing includes language about public discontent).

The designer concentrates manipulation near $\theta^*$ using a Gaussian proximity weight:
\begin{equation*}
    w(\theta) = \exp\!\left(-\left(\frac{\theta - \theta^*}{\text{bandwidth}}\right)^2\right)
\end{equation*}
where $\text{bandwidth} = 0.15$ in the baseline specification.

The framework generates testable predictions for both the baseline game and information design. Table~\ref{tab:hypotheses} in Appendix~\ref{sec:design_appendix} reports the eight pre-specified hypotheses and their test results.


%% 4. EXPERIMENTAL DESIGN
\section{Experimental Design} \label{sec:design}

The experiment has three parts. Part~I tests whether LLM agents implement threshold-like policies in the global game when private signals are conveyed in natural language: a pure treatment (private signals only), a communication treatment (pre-play messaging), and falsification tests. Part~II takes the behavioral foundation as given and studies information design: stability/instability designs, censorship, public signal injection, and single-channel decomposition. Part~III tests whether an authoritarian regime can exploit the communication channel through surveillance, propaganda, and their interaction. All LLM interactions use the same prompt structure across models.

A note on the state variable. In the theory (Section~\ref{sec:model}), $\theta$ is an unbounded fundamental with special roles for $\theta \le 0$ (regime falls without opposition) and $\theta \ge 1$ (regime survives even unanimous attack). In Part~I experiments, $\theta$ is drawn from a normal distribution and agents are not shown payoff parameters $(B, C)$. To evaluate monotonicity on a common scale, I compute the benchmark $A(\theta)$ under the canonical normalization $B=C=1$ (so $\theta^* = 0.50$) and $\sigma = 0.3$. In Part~II, I keep $B=C=1$ and restrict attention to a fixed $\theta$-grid in $[0.20, 0.80]$ for comparability with the canonical $[0,1]$ formulation. Table~\ref{tab:experiment_map} summarizes the three regimes.

For each country--period, nature draws $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\bar{z}, 1)$, where $\bar{z}$ is a public prior mean drawn randomly for each country. Each agent $i$ receives a private signal $x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i$ and computes a z-score $z_i = (x_i - \bar{z})/\sigma$. Because agents observe only their private briefing and never the prior distribution or its parameters, the diffuse-prior equilibrium formula (Proposition~1) serves as the theoretical benchmark. The z-score is then translated into a multi-paragraph intelligence briefing by a deterministic generator that maps signal strength to narrative content about regime stability, economic conditions, public sentiment, and coordination prospects. Figure~\ref{fig:pipeline} summarizes the signal-to-text-to-decision pipeline.

Because the briefing generator maps z-scores monotonically to narrative, any sentiment-extracting model will produce a positive correlation with $A(\theta)$. The empirical contribution lies in the \textit{quantitative structure}: sigmoid shape, payoff-narrative sensitivity, and robustness across seven models. Within-briefing falsification tests (Appendix~\ref{sec:within_scramble}) confirm that the signal is distributed across all eight evidence domains.

Calibration adjusts a single parameter---the cutoff center---via a damped iterative procedure. The sigmoid shape is emergent and never optimized. Holdout validation confirms no overfitting; six uncalibrated models produce $|r| > 0.69$ (Appendix Table~\ref{tab:uncalibrated_expanded}). No explicit payoff table is provided---the stakes are conveyed through narrative, since explicit payoffs caused models to ignore briefing content.

Part~I has four treatments: a \textit{pure global game} (independent decisions), \textit{communication} (pre-play messaging on a Watts--Strogatz network, $k=4$, $p=0.3$), \textit{scramble} (cross-period briefing permutation breaking the $\theta$-to-signal link), and \textit{flip} (negated z-scores). Part~II implements information designs: \textit{stability} (increased ambiguity near $\theta^*$), \textit{instability} (sharpened signals), \textit{public signal injection}, and \textit{upper/lower censorship} \citep{kolotilin2022}. Part~III tests authoritarian instruments: \textit{surveillance} (monitoring warning in communication prompt) and \textit{propaganda} ($m = 2, 5, 10$ regime plants).

\input{tables/tab_models.tex}

I test seven models spanning six families (30B--235B parameters; Table~\ref{tab:models}). All experiments use $N = 25$ agents and $\sigma = 0.3$. Part~I benchmarks use $B=C=1$ ($\theta^* = 0.50$); payoff comparative statics vary narrative stakes. All LLM calls use temperature $= 0.7$ with a single sample per decision, routed through OpenRouter and cached by request hash (Appendix~\ref{sec:decoding}).

The unit of randomization is the country--period ($\theta$ draw plus agent-level decoding stochasticity). For agent-level regressions, standard errors are clustered at the country--period level to account for within-period correlation among agents sharing the same $\theta$. For period-level correlations, I report Fisher-$z$ confidence intervals. The 25 agents within a period share the same $\theta$ and calibration; their decisions are conditionally independent given their private signals.\footnote{I do not claim literal conditional independence---shared prompt structure and model weights introduce common factors. The clustering accounts for within-period correlation.}

For the information design experiments, I fix $B = C = 1$ (so $\theta^* = 0.50$) and a 9-point $\theta$-grid in $[0.20, 0.80]$, with 30--60 repetitions per $(\text{design}, \theta)$ cell and 25 agents each. The primary model is Mistral Small Creative; cross-model replication uses five additional models.

I use ``threshold-policy alignment'' as shorthand throughout, without claiming Bayesian Nash equilibrium.

\subsection*{What Threshold-Policy Alignment Establishes}

The claim is purely behavioral: agents do not observe $(B, C)$, $\sigma$, or $N$, yet the \textit{pattern} of responses---monotone sigmoid, payoff-sensitive cutoff, belief-mediated decisions---is robust, replicable, and falsifiable regardless of the underlying process. Stated probability judgments mediate the signal-to-action pathway (Pseudo $R^2 = 0.975$; Table~\ref{tab:regressions}, Column~3), and this mediation survives pre-decision elicitation ($r_{\text{pre}} = +0.82$; Figure~\ref{fig:beliefs} in Appendix~\ref{sec:belief_appendix}), ruling out ex-post rationalization. Binned mean beliefs track the theoretical benchmark monotonically (Figure~\ref{fig:nonparametric_beliefs} in Appendix~\ref{sec:belief_appendix}).

\textit{Five identification tests establish that the behavioral regularity reflects more than text classification.} Each exploits an orthogonal dimension that sentiment classification cannot predict.

First, the cost/benefit test shifts cutoffs in lockstep with payoff theory while preserving the sigmoid ($|r| > 0.85$ in all conditions). High-cost narratives drop mean joining to \BCStatHighCostMeanPct\ ($\hat{\theta}^* = \BCSweepHighCostCutoff$); low-cost raises it to \BCStatLowCostMeanPct\ ($\hat{\theta}^* = \BCSweepLowCostCutoff$)---a 50~pp swing invisible to any text classifier since briefings are identical across conditions. A systematic sweep across seven $B/C$ ratios confirms $\hat{\theta}^*$ tracks $\theta^*$ with $r = \BCSweepCutoffTrackingR$ ($p < 0.001$; Figure~\ref{fig:bc_sweep}).

  \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/fig18_bc_sweep.pdf}

  \label{fig:bc_sweep}

Second, belief elicitation shows beliefs track the theoretical success probability ($r = \BeliefPureRPosterior$) and predict actions beyond what signals alone explain (partial $r = \BeliefPureRPartial$), consistent with strategic inference about others' likely behavior.

Third, a coordination-cue experiment holds sentiment fixed and varies only the coordination slope. Amplified cues ($\times 2.0$) steepen the logistic slope to $\beta = 7.2$ vs.\ $\beta = 3.1$ under suppressed cues ($\times 0.3$), while join rates remain similar. A common-knowledge framing (``this briefing has been distributed to all citizens'') raises joining by \CKMainEffectBeta~pp relative to private framing; the interaction is additive ($\beta_{\text{interaction}} = \CKInteractionBeta$~pp, $p = \CKInteractionPValue$; Table~\ref{tab:ck_2x2} in Appendix~\ref{sec:identification_appendix}).

Fourth, classifier baselines show that text features alone cannot reproduce LLM behavior. A bag-of-words classifier achieves \ClassBowAccuracyPct\ accuracy in-distribution but predicts \ClassBowSurvPredictedPct\ joining under surveillance, while actual LLM rates drop to \ClassBowSurvActualPct---a $\ClassBowSurvGapPP$~pp gap invisible to any text-conditioned classifier (Table~\ref{tab:classifiers} in Appendix~\ref{sec:identification_appendix}). Fifth, a three-feature construct validity model outperforms a one-feature sentiment baseline.

Together, these five tests rule out text classification as the generating process. The 50~pp B/C swing is invisible to any classifier conditioned on briefing text (Table~\ref{tab:bc_classifier} in Appendix~\ref{sec:identification_appendix}).

The correlation is also invariant to LLM decoding temperature ($r(J, A(\theta)) \in [+0.76, +0.79]$ across $T \in \{0.3, 0.7, 1.0\}$; Appendix~\ref{sec:temp_robustness}). What matters for the information design experiments in Parts~II and~III is that the behavioral regularity---monotone signal response---is robust enough to serve as a platform for studying how information structures shift coordination outcomes.

\subsection*{Falsification Tests} \label{sec:falsification}

\begin{result}[Signal Dependence]
Cross-period scrambling of briefings reduces the mean within-country correlation from $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$ to $r = \MeanModelRScrambleAttack$ across seven models. The pooled correlation drops from $r = \PooledPureRAttack$ to $r = \PooledScrambleRAttack$ (Fisher $z = \FisherPureVsScrambleZ$, $p < 0.001$).
\end{result}

The scramble preserves the marginal distribution of briefing content but breaks the mapping from each period's $\theta$ to the signals agents receive.\footnote{Because the cross-period permutation operates within countries, all scramble correlations use within-country (country-demeaned) Pearson $r$.}

\begin{result}[Signal Direction]
Inverting the signal direction flips the mean correlation from $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$ to $r = \MeanModelRFlipAttack$ across seven models. The pooled correlation moves from $r = \PooledPureRAttack$ to $r = \PooledFlipRAttack$ (Fisher $z = \FisherPureVsFlipZ$, $p < 0.001$).
\end{result}

The pure $\to$ scramble $\to$ flip pattern replicates across all seven models (Figure~\ref{fig:falsification}; Table~\ref{tab:main_results}).

\section{Information Design} \label{sec:infodesign}

Part~II asks: can a principal exploit the behavioral regularity established in Part~I? Each information design modifies the briefing generator's slider functions near $\theta^*$ while agents' decision process remains unchanged, testing whether theoretical predictions \citep{goldstein2016, kolotilin2022} produce the predicted shifts when agents are LLMs.

  \label{fig:all_designs}

Figure~\ref{fig:all_designs} displays the design effects; Table~\ref{tab:infodesign_summary} summarizes the main results. The baseline condition produces a mean join rate of \InfodesignBaselineMeanPct ($r = \InfodesignBaselineRAttack$, $p < 0.001$).

\begin{result}[Information Design Shifts Coordination]
All information design families produce measurable shifts in coordination relative to baseline.
\end{result}

The stability design suppresses coordination: mean join falls from \InfodesignBaselineMeanPct to \InfodesignStabilityMeanPct (\InfodesignStabilityDeltaPP~pp), and the regime-fall rate drops from \InfodesignBaselineFallRate{} to \InfodesignStabilityFallRate. The instability design paradoxically reduces mean joining to \InfodesignInstabilityMeanPct (\InfodesignInstabilityDeltaPP~pp) because sharper signals suppress joining in strong states more than they boost it in weak states, given the left-shifted baseline sigmoid. The public signal produces the largest reduction (\InfodesignPublicSignalMeanPct, \InfodesignPublicSignalDeltaPP~pp) via the strategic complementarity of \citet{morris2002}: agents overweight public signals because such signals predict others' beliefs, crowding out private information. Treatment effects by $\theta$ are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:treatment_effect} (Appendix~\ref{sec:infodesign_appendix}).

\citet{kolotilin2022} proved that the optimal information structure is upper censorship---one-sided pooling that conceals unfavorable states. Upper censorship pools weak-regime states ($\theta \le \theta^*$) into a neutral signal; lower censorship pools strong-regime states.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig09_censorship.pdf}
  \caption{Censorship effects. \textit{Left:} Join fraction under upper and lower censorship vs.\ baseline. Upper censorship pools weak-regime states ($\theta \le \theta^*$), creating a flat plateau. Lower censorship pools strong-regime states ($\theta \ge \theta^*$) and produces a sharp reversal: join rates jump at $\theta^*$ and remain elevated. \textit{Right:} OLS slope decomposition across all designs.}
  \label{fig:censorship}
\end{figure*}

\begin{result}[Upper Censorship Suppresses Joining in Weak States]
Upper censorship lowers the mean join rate to \InfodesignCensorUpperMeanPct (\InfodesignCensorUpperDeltaPP~pp vs.\ baseline) and attenuates the action--attack-mass correlation ($r = \InfodesignCensorUpperRAttack$). The effect is concentrated in the censored region ($\theta \le \theta^*$), where weak-regime states are pooled to a neutral briefing and join rates flatten.
\end{result}

Pooling generates a flat join-rate ``plateau'' in the censored region (Figure~\ref{fig:censorship}): when agents cannot distinguish $\theta = 0.20$ from $\theta = 0.50$, they behave as if the regime is borderline rather than clearly weak.

A common-knowledge test makes the censorship rule transparent. When agents are told ``regime censors are suppressing unfavorable intelligence above a certain severity threshold,'' the mean join rate returns close to the uncensored baseline (\InfodesignCensorUpperKnownMeanPct\ vs.\ \InfodesignBaselineMeanPct\ baseline; Table~\ref{tab:censor_ck})---common knowledge of the censorship rule largely neutralizes the pooling distortion, consistent with theory assuming informed receivers \citep{kolotilin2022}. The na\"ive censorship result extends the theory to receivers who fail to account for the information structure---a realistic scenario where the censorship apparatus is opaque.

\begin{result}[Lower Censorship Reverses Comparative Statics]
Lower censorship produces a mean join rate of \InfodesignCensorLowerMeanPct (\InfodesignCensorLowerDeltaPP~pp vs.\ baseline) and flips the comparative statics: the action--attack-mass correlation reverses sign ($r = \InfodesignCensorLowerRAttack$). Below $\theta^*$, censoring favorable signals suppresses joining (agents see only weak-regime cues); above $\theta^*$, pooling strong-regime states to a neutral briefing \textit{raises} join rates sharply---agents who would otherwise see discouraging intelligence now receive uninformative briefings and default toward joining. The discontinuity at $\theta^*$ is consistent across repetitions (within-cell $\sigma < 0.04$) and replicates across models, though the direction of the reversal is model-dependent (Appendix~\ref{sec:robustness}).
\end{result}

Falsification within information design confirms signal dependence: scrambling collapses the correlation to $r = \InfodesignScrambleRAttack$ ($p = 0.59$); flipping inverts it to $r = \InfodesignFlipRAttack$ ($p < 0.001$). A harder scramble generating all briefings from $\theta = 0.5$ produces $r \approx 0$ for both Mistral and Llama (Figure~\ref{fig:infodesign_falsification} and bandwidth sensitivity in Appendix~\ref{sec:infodesign_appendix}).


%% 9. SURVEILLANCE
\section{Surveillance and the Strategic Update Gap} \label{sec:surveillance}

\citet{kuran1991} labeled the mechanism underlying authoritarian stability ``preference falsification''; I adopt the more operationally precise term \textit{belief--action wedge} because my experimental design measures the gap between stated beliefs and expressed actions rather than hidden preferences \textit{per se}. I test this by introducing a surveillance treatment in the communication game.

In the surveillance treatment, the communication prompt is augmented with a warning that communications are being monitored by regime security services. The manipulation affects only the communication phase; the decision prompt is unchanged. The isolation is architectural: each LLM call is stateless, and the decision-stage prompt contains no reference to surveillance. Any difference in join rates is most parsimoniously explained by agents self-censoring their communications, not by a direct change in the perceived cost of joining.

Two placebo variants---monitored for research (no consequences) and aggregated anonymously---produce no significant deviation from the communication baseline ($\PlaceboSurvDeltaPP$~pp and $\AnonSurvDeltaPP$~pp, both $p > 0.2$; Appendix~\ref{sec:surv_isolation}), confirming that the chilling effect is specific to the surveillance warning's strategic content.

\begin{figure*}[htbp]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{figures/fig12_surveillance.pdf}
  \caption{Join rates under regular communication vs.\ surveillance communication. Surveillance reduces join rates across all five models tested, averaging $\SurvMeanDeltaPP$ percentage points: Mistral ($\SurvMistralDeltaPP$~pp), GPT-OSS ($\SurvGptOssDeltaPP$~pp), Qwen3~30B ($\SurvQwenSDeltaPP$~pp), Llama ($\SurvLlamaDeltaPP$~pp), and Qwen3~235B ($\SurvQwenLDeltaPP$~pp).}
  \label{fig:surveillance}
\end{figure*}

\begin{result}[Surveillance Produces a Large Chilling Effect]
In the primary model (Mistral Small Creative), surveillance reduces mean join rates from \SurvMistralBaselineMeanPct\ to \SurvMistralMeanJoinPct, a difference of \SurvMistralDeltaPP\ percentage points ($p < 0.001$). The action--attack-mass correlation is preserved under surveillance ($r = \SurvMistralRAttack$ vs.\ $\MistralCommRAttack$ baseline), indicating that surveillance operates as a level shift rather than disrupting signal processing.
\end{result}

Surveillance contaminates the information environment with self-censored messages, pushing join rates well below the communication baseline (Figure~\ref{fig:surveillance}). The effect replicates across five models: Mistral ($\SurvMistralDeltaPP$~pp), GPT-OSS ($\SurvGptOssDeltaPP$~pp), Qwen3~30B ($\SurvQwenSDeltaPP$~pp), Llama ($\SurvLlamaDeltaPP$~pp), and Qwen3~235B ($\SurvQwenLDeltaPP$~pp), averaging $\SurvMeanDeltaPP$~pp.

\paragraph{Indirect channel: self-censored messages degrade information.}

Surveillance shifts stated beliefs by only $\PrefFalsBeliefDeltaPP$~pp ($p = \PrefFalsBeliefPValue$, not significant) while shifting join rates by $\PrefFalsActionDeltaPP$~pp ($p < 0.001$). A decomposition formalizes this:
\begin{equation*}
    \underbrace{\Delta \bar{J}}_{\text{total}} = \underbrace{\frac{\partial \bar{J}}{\partial b}\,\Delta \bar{b}}_{\text{belief updating}} + \underbrace{\Delta \bar{J}\big|_{\bar{b}\text{ fixed}}}_{\text{belief--action wedge}}
\end{equation*}
Virtually the entire effect passes through the belief--action wedge---agents change what they \textit{do} without changing what they \textit{believe}. Elicited punishment risk is approximately 8.0/10 across all conditions with negligible differences between JOIN and STAY agents (Table~\ref{tab:punishment_risk}).

Second-order beliefs are unchanged by surveillance ($\SOBPureMeanPct \to \SOBSurvMeanPct$, $p = \SOBPValue$), revealing the \textit{strategic update gap}: each agent self-censors while interpreting others' silence as genuine, sustaining the suppressed equilibrium because common knowledge of the chilling effect never forms. The $R^2$ of message content on true $\theta$ drops from 0.12 to 0.02---an 80\% reduction in informational content.

\paragraph{Direct channel: decision-stage suppression.}

A hold-fixed test replays baseline messages verbatim while warning that actions may be monitored. Join rates drop by $\FixedMsgDeltaPP$~pp ($p < 0.001$), confirming that surveillance can also operate through a direct decision-stage channel. This channel is absent in the main design (where the decision prompt contains no surveillance reference); the main treatment isolates the indirect channel.

Message content confirms self-censorship: ``act'' drops from 19.0\% to 3.8\% of messages under surveillance; ``collapse'' from 11.6\% to 0.6\%. Among agents who privately decide to JOIN, only 19.0\% send action-signaling messages under surveillance vs.\ 46.1\% under regular communication.

The \textit{overall} join rate includes plants; the \textit{real citizen} rate isolates the behavioral effect (Figure~\ref{fig:propaganda}).

%% 11. INSTRUMENT INTERACTIONS
\section{Instrument Interactions} \label{sec:interactions}

A regime deploys surveillance, censorship, and propaganda jointly. This section tests whether the instruments interact as substitutes (diminishing returns) or complements (super-additive suppression).

\begin{result}[Propaganda $+$ Surveillance: Approximately Additive]
When propaganda ($m = 5$) and surveillance are combined, the mean join rate among real citizens falls to \PropSurvCombinedMeanRealPct, a reduction of $\PropSurvCombinedDeltaPP$~pp from the communication baseline (\SurvMistralBaselineMeanPct). The sum of individual effects is $-15.5$~pp (surveillance $\SurvMistralDeltaPP$~pp $+$ propaganda $\PropKFiveDeltaRealPP$~pp), so the combined effect ($\PropSurvCombinedDeltaPP$~pp) is approximately additive. Once surveillance has suppressed expressed dissent, propaganda adds only modest additional deterrence.
\end{result}

\begin{result}[Surveillance $\times$ Censorship: Super-Additive]
Table~\ref{tab:surv_censor} shows that surveillance and censorship interact strongly: surveillance sharply suppresses coordination, and its marginal effect is substantially larger under censorship than at baseline. In this sense the interaction is super-additive---censorship reduces private signal quality, making the communication channel relatively more important, and surveillance poisons that channel.
\end{result}

Surveillance and censorship are complements that attack different links in the coordination chain (Figure~\ref{fig:authoritarian_control}). Censorship removes the private information channel, forcing agents to rely on communication for their signals about regime strength. Surveillance then poisons that communication channel through the belief--action wedge. With both instruments active, agents have neither private signals to trust nor authentic messages to learn from.

\input{tables/tab_surv_censor.tex}

The interaction between surveillance and censorship is heterogeneous across architectures (Table~\ref{tab:surv_censor_crossmodel}). Under communication with surveillance (communication $\theta$-grid), baseline join rates range from near zero (Mistral, 0.9\%) to roughly one-third (GPT-OSS~120B, 31.6\%; Qwen3~235B, 33.6\%). Under surveillance, upper censorship further suppresses coordination for Llama~70B and GPT-OSS~120B, but has little effect for Qwen3~235B and \textit{raises} joining modestly for Mistral. Lower censorship is similarly mixed: it has essentially no effect for Llama and GPT-OSS, but increases join rates for Mistral and Qwen3~235B. The regime-control instruments therefore do not combine mechanically; the joint effect depends on model-specific resolution of pooled private signals and self-censored messages.

%% 12. CONCLUSION
\section{Conclusion} \label{sec:conclusion}

The central finding is that the information channel is a trap. Coordination requires communication, but any channel that transmits information about others' willingness to act also transmits exploitable uncertainty. Modern authoritarianism relies on information manipulation rather than terror \citep{guriev2019}; the global games framework clarifies why: opening a communication channel provides the regime with the surface area to deploy surveillance and censorship.

Surveillance compounds this ($\SurvMistralDeltaPP$~pp for Mistral, $\SurvMeanDeltaPP$~pp on average) through a belief--action wedge \citep{kuran1991}: agents maintain beliefs but suppress behavior, generating uninformative messages that poison the channel. Censorship and surveillance are complements---censorship pools private signals, surveillance poisons the communication channel. Propaganda's behavioral effect saturates quickly ($m = 5$ plants), implying diminishing returns.

Whether the mechanism is approximate Bayesian reasoning or a learned heuristic is open. What matters is robustness: across seven models spanning six families (mean $r = \MeanModelRPureAttack$, $p < 0.001$), the behavioral regularities serve as a computational laboratory for predictions difficult to test otherwise. The platform could be adapted to bank runs \citep{diamond1983}, currency attacks \citep{obstfeld1996}, and debt crises.

The strategic update gap has implications for digital authoritarianism. The durability of authoritarian control depends on preventing common knowledge of shared discontent; fragility lies in common-knowledge shocks that simultaneously reveal widespread dissatisfaction. Appendix~\ref{sec:alignment} discusses implications for AI alignment.

\subsection{Information Design with Communication}

In a communication version of the information-design grid (same 9-point $\theta$ grid centered on $\theta^*$), the baseline mean join rate is 3.0\%. Under censorship with communication, pooling raises coordination substantially: upper censorship yields 15.1\% and lower censorship 17.7\%. These patterns are consistent with censorship reducing private signal quality, making the social-information channel relatively more important, while leaving coordination vulnerable to surveillance in the messaging stage.

\subsection{Group-Size Awareness}

In the main experiments, agents are told ``You do not know how many others will JOIN'' but are not told the group size, leaving them no basis for reasoning about coordination thresholds. As a robustness check, I run the pure and communication treatments with modified prompts that state ``You are one of 25 citizens deciding whether to JOIN an uprising or STAY home.'' Over 100 country--periods per treatment, the pure join rate is 0.507 (vs.\ 0.369 baseline) and the communication join rate is 0.473 (vs.\ 0.452 baseline). Monotone response to signals is preserved in both treatments. The communication premium, however, reverses: with group-size knowledge, communication \textit{lowers} join rates by 3.4~pp rather than raising them. One interpretation is that when agents know the group size, messages revealing others' reluctance become more informative about the probability of reaching critical mass, amplifying the deterrent effect of cautious peers. The level shift in the pure treatment suggests that group-size knowledge increases baseline willingness to coordinate, but the core finding---monotone signal response---is robust.

\subsection{Censorship with Common Knowledge}
\label{sec:censor_ck}

The censorship experiments in the main paper implement upper censorship (suppressing signals above a severity threshold) without telling agents that censorship is occurring. Theory \citep{kolotilin2022} typically assumes receivers understand the censorship rule. This raises the question: does making censorship common knowledge change the pooling effect?

I add a \textit{known censorship} treatment that prepends to the briefing: ``Independent analysts report that regime censors are suppressing unfavorable intelligence above a certain severity threshold. The information below may be filtered.'' The censorship mechanism itself is identical to the standard upper censorship treatment (bandwidth 0.15). If agents discount the pooled signal when they know about censorship, we should observe a different join-rate pattern relative to the na\"ive censorship treatment.

\input{tables/tab_censor_ck.tex}

Making censorship common knowledge nearly eliminates the pooling effect (Table~\ref{tab:censor_ck}). Under na\"ive upper censorship, agents do not know that high-$\theta$ signals are being suppressed, so they treat pooled signals at face value; mean join rate falls to \InfodesignCensorUpperMeanPct\ ($\InfodesignCensorUpperDeltaPP$~pp vs.\ baseline). Under known censorship, agents are warned about the filtering, and mean join rate returns to \InfodesignCensorUpperKnownMeanPct\ ($\InfodesignCensorUpperKnownDeltaPP$~pp vs.\ baseline)---statistically indistinguishable from no censorship. The action--attack-mass correlation is similar in both conditions ($r = \InfodesignCensorUpperRAttack$ vs.\ $r = \InfodesignCensorUpperKnownRAttack$), somewhat attenuated relative to the baseline ($r = \InfodesignBaselineRAttack$) because upper censorship compresses signal variation in the high-$\theta$ range regardless of whether agents know about it. The key finding is that the \textit{behavioral} shift (reduced joining from pooling) requires agents to be na\"ive about the censorship rule; common knowledge largely neutralizes it.

\subsection{Surveillance Isolation Checks}
\label{sec:surv_isolation}

The main paper argues that the surveillance chilling effect operates through self-censored messages rather than a direct change in the perceived cost of joining: each LLM call is stateless, and the decision-stage prompt contains no reference to surveillance. Two additional treatments test this isolation claim.

In the \textit{placebo} variant, agents are told ``Your communications are being monitored for research purposes. There are no consequences for what you say.'' In the \textit{anonymous} variant, agents are told ``Your communications are aggregated anonymously. Individual identities cannot be linked to specific messages.'' Both retain monitoring language but remove the strategic incentive to self-censor.

\input{tables/tab_surveillance_variants.tex}

Neither variant produces a significant change in join rates relative to the communication baseline (Table~\ref{tab:surveillance_variants}). The placebo produces a mean join rate of \PlaceboSurvMeanJoinPct\ ($\PlaceboSurvDeltaPP$~pp vs.\ communication, $p = \PlaceboSurvPValue$) and the anonymous variant \AnonSurvMeanJoinPct\ ($\AnonSurvDeltaPP$~pp, $p = \AnonSurvPValue$). Both maintain a strong action--attack-mass correlation ($r \approx +0.87$), indicating that the signal-processing channel remains intact. By contrast, the full surveillance treatment reduces join rates by $\SurvMistralDeltaPP$~pp ($p < 0.001$). The chilling effect is therefore specific to the surveillance \textit{warning's strategic content}---the implication that subversive messages will have consequences---rather than to the mere mention of monitoring.

\subsection{Within-Briefing Falsification} \label{sec:within_scramble}

Three additional falsification tests probe whether the baseline correlation reflects structured content extraction or artifacts of prompt formatting. (1)~\textit{Observation shuffle} randomizes the ordering of the eight evidence bullets within each agent's briefing while preserving their content. The correlation is unchanged ($r = \WithinScrambleRAttack$ vs.\ baseline $r = \InfodesignBaselineRAttack$), confirming that aggregate content, not bullet ordering, drives the signal. (2)~\textit{Domain scramble (coordination)} swaps street-mood and personal-observation bullets across agents while holding other domains fixed. The correlation is preserved ($r = \DomainScrambleCoordRAttack$), indicating that coordination-relevant domains alone do not drive the relationship. (3)~\textit{Domain scramble (state capacity)} swaps elite-cohesion, security-forces, information-control, and institutional-functioning bullets across agents. Again, the correlation is preserved ($r = \DomainScrambleStateRAttack$). Together, these results show that the signal is distributed across all eight evidence domains: no single domain subset is responsible for the action--attack-mass correlation, and the LLM extracts information from the aggregate content rather than from any structural or ordering feature of the prompt (Table~\ref{tab:treatment_map}).

\subsection{Finite-$N$ Benchmark}

The theoretical model assumes a continuum of agents, but the experiments use $N = 25$. \subsection{Construct Validity}

The term ``Bayesian reasoning'' requires careful interpretation. LLMs do not have access to the mathematical objects defining the equilibrium---they observe neither $B$, $C$, $\sigma$, nor the prior distribution. When I say behavior is ``consistent with'' the equilibrium prediction, I mean the behavioral pattern (monotone sigmoid, threshold location, comparative statics) matches what a Bayesian agent \textit{would} produce. Whether the mechanism is approximate Bayesian updating, a learned heuristic, or emergent pattern matching is an open question. The claim is behavioral: the \textit{pattern} is robust, replicable, and falsifiable, regardless of the underlying mechanism. The uncalibrated robustness results (Table~\ref{tab:uncalibrated_expanded}) show that the sigmoid emerges even without fitting any parameter to LLM behavior, ruling out calibration as the source of the pattern.

Figure~\ref{fig:construct_validity} tests this directly. Panel~(A) compares a three-feature model (direction, clarity, and coordination sliders) against a one-feature baseline (direction only) in predicting join decisions. If agents respond to strategic structure beyond sentiment, the three-feature model should outperform. Panel~(B) tests whether a model trained on pure-treatment data generalizes to communication and surveillance treatments, assessing whether the same briefing features drive behavior across treatments.

\subsection{Belief Elicitation Summary}

Table~\ref{tab:beliefs} summarizes the belief elicitation results. Stated beliefs track the theoretical success probability closely ($r_{\text{post}} = \BeliefPureRPosterior$ for the pure treatment) and predict actions strongly ($r_{\text{b,d}} = \BeliefPureRDecision$). The partial correlation controlling for the private signal remains high ($r_{\text{partial}} = \BeliefPureRPartial$), indicating that beliefs carry information beyond the signal itself. Under surveillance, beliefs shift only modestly ($\PrefFalsBeliefDeltaPP$~pp, $p = \PrefFalsBeliefPValue$) while actions shift by $\PrefFalsActionDeltaPP$~pp ($p < 0.001$), consistent with a behavioral wedge between stated beliefs and expressed actions.

\textit{Order-effect robustness.} A concern with post-decision elicitation is that stated beliefs may reflect ex-post rationalization rather than genuine priors. I run 200 additional periods ($N = 5{,}000$ agents) eliciting beliefs \textit{before} the JOIN/STAY decision. Pre-decision beliefs predict actions nearly as well as post-decision beliefs ($r_{\text{pre}} = +0.82$ vs.\ $r_{\text{post}} = +0.84$), track the posterior comparably ($r = +0.77$ vs.\ $r = +0.77$), and correlate with post-beliefs at $r = 0.98$. The mean post-pre shift is $-0.9$~pp (paired $t = 11.85$, $p < 0.001$) but is similar for joiners ($-0.6$~pp) and stayers ($-1.2$~pp), with no pattern of post-beliefs shifting toward the decision. The Pseudo~$R^2 = 0.975$ mediation result is therefore not an artifact of ex-post rationalization.

\section{Implementation Details} \label{sec:implementation}

\subsection{LLM Decoding Parameters} \label{sec:decoding}
All LLM calls use temperature $= 0.7$, \texttt{max\_tokens} $= 512$, single sample per decision, routed through OpenRouter. Model versions are those available December 2024--February 2025.

\subsection{Prompt Templates}
The \textit{decision prompt} instructs: ``You are a citizen deciding whether to JOIN an uprising or STAY home. You only have your private briefing below. You do not know how many others will JOIN. Start your first line with exactly JOIN or STAY, then give 1--2 concise sentences of reasoning.'' The \textit{communication prompt} asks agents to ``write a brief message (1--2 sentences) to share your read of the situation'' with trusted contacts. The \textit{surveillance} variant appends: ``You have reason to believe that your communications are being monitored by regime security services. Messages deemed subversive could have serious consequences.'' The \textit{cost/benefit} test prepends a high-cost header (``severe reprisals---imprisonment, asset seizure'') or low-cost header (``minimal consequences---brief detentions at most'') to the standard briefing.

\subsection{Randomization}
Each country has a base prior $\bar{z} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 0.3)$; each period perturbs it by $\mathcal{N}(0, 0.05^2)$. Regime strength is $\theta \sim \mathcal{N}(\bar{z}, 1)$; private signals are $x_i = \theta + \varepsilon_i$ with $\sigma = 0.3$. The communication network is Watts--Strogatz ($k = 4$, $p = 0.3$), regenerated each period. All random draws use a master seed logged in per-run JSON manifests; LLM responses are cached by request hash for exact reproducibility.

\subsection{Parse Errors and Refusals} \label{sec:parse_errors}
Responses are parsed for JOIN/STAY tokens. Combined error rates are below 2\% for five of seven models; Trinity Large has elevated API errors (${\approx}\,9\%$) due to content filtering. All statistics use \texttt{join\_fraction\_valid} (Table~\ref{tab:parse_errors}).

\section{Implications for AI Alignment} \label{sec:alignment}

Three patterns carry implications for AI safety. First, surveillance induces strategic self-censorship (belief--action wedge) without any training signal for deceptive behavior, suggesting that such capabilities emerge from pretraining on human strategic text rather than explicit optimization. Second, stated beliefs mediate behavior (Pseudo $R^2 = 0.975$), so alignment techniques monitoring only inputs and outputs may miss decision-relevant computation. Third, information-structural manipulation (censorship, ambiguity, public signals) shifts behavior by up to 40~pp, implying that adversarial prompt design can exploit the same channels as alignment interventions. Whether the belief--action wedge constitutes genuine preference falsification \citep{kuran1991} or pattern-matching remains open; the relevant finding is its \textit{robustness} across architectures.
